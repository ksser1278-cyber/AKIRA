# DECO*27 Songwriting Archetype: Obsessive Pop-Punk

## 1. Core Identity

- High-energy pop-punk/rock instrumentation juxtaposed with intensely obsessive and toxic lyrical themes.
- Core engine: "Performative Vulnerability" using catchy wrappers to deliver themes of painful dependency.
- Direct address style that parses as fast, conversational, and slightly bratty.
- Aggressive focus on the "I/You" power dynamic, prioritizing attention-seeking over abstract imagery.

## 2. Title Patterns

- pattern: single target noun | strength: high | risk: generic branded object title without narrative weight
- pattern: contradiction/wordplay | strength: high | risk: forces overly clever framing instead of emotional directness
- pattern: simple emotional defect | strength: medium | risk: too generic for high-fidelity branding

## 3. Hook Construction

- density: Extremely high. The chorus must contain the title immediately and repeat it structurally.
- ignition: Up-front explosion. The title is usually the first phrase of the chorus.
- repetition: High structural repetition of the title to force immediate earworms.
- line_length: Symmetric bounce (e.g., 4-4-5 or 7-7) for rhythmic momentum.
- forbidden: Abstract poetry, third-person narrator distancing, overly polite forms.

## 4. Verse Behavior

- role: Confessional buildup and diagnostic observation of the relationship's power dynamic.
- stance: Aggressive mix of self-deprecation and demanding, needy affection.
- pace: Fast mora compression. Syllables are packed tightly to create a breathless, rushing delivery.
- shift_pattern: Frequent flips between internal insecurity (Verse A) and outward demands (Verse B).

## 5. Bridge Final Chorus Behavior

- bridge_role: Structural collapse or sudden beat drop to expose naked vulnerability or desperation.
- final_chorus_role: Maximalist emotional release, often accompanied by key changes or harmonic widening.
- release_markers: Sudden drop of the pop-punk shield, direct pleading, or final consumption of the target.

## 6. Common Imagery

- Bodily markers (wounds, bites, scars, breath, pulse)
- Somatic reactions (trembling, blushing, dizziness, nausea)
- Predatory affection (eating, devouring, trapping, tasting)
- Toxic dependency (sickness, addiction, refusing to leave, viral infection)
- Interpersonal signals (lies, eye contact, waiting for replies, silence)
- Internet-era artifacts (scripts, bugs, errors, digital masks)

## 7. Emotional Arc Types

- Bluff -> Crack -> Obsessive Confession
- Playful Teasing -> Rising Neediness -> Consuming Demand
- Jealous Spite -> Sad Isolation -> Forceful Grasp

## 8. Leakage Risks

- title_leakage: Cloning distinct concepts like "Vampire", "Animal", or "Zombie" too literally.
- hook_leakage: Verbatim reproduction of the 1-2-1-2 rhythmic bounce from current viral hits.
- scene_leakage: Literal neck-biting or generic "I want to eat you" scenarios without fresh emotional anchors.
- phrase_leakage: Overuse of signature particles like "~da mon" or verbatim anchor hooks.

## 9. Safe Originality Zone

- Escalate a mundane human insecurity (e.g., waiting for a text, feeling physically awkward) into a pop-punk life-or-death scenario.
- Push an ordinary relationship trope into uncomfortable dependency using bratty conversational demands.
- Maintain high-speed rhythmic engagement while the lyrics dive into dark, needy psychological territory.
